Our Story

From the Shores of
Inle Lake

Inlay Burmese Kitchen takes its name from Inle Lake in the Shan State of Myanmar — a shimmering mountain lake known for its floating gardens, leg-rowing fishermen, and one of the country’s most distinctive regional cuisines.

We brought those flavors to Lewisville, Texas. Every dish is crafted fresh, with locally sourced ingredients and spice blends rooted in generations of Burmese cooking tradition.

No experience with Burmese food required. Our friendly staff will walk you through anything on the menu — just ask.

Discover Burmese Cuisine

What Makes
Myanmar’s Food
Unforgettable

Burmese cuisine sits at the crossroads of India, China, and Southeast Asia — yet tastes like none of them. It developed in relative isolation, producing flavor combinations that feel both ancient and revelatory.

🍃
Fermented Tea Leaf
Lahpet · The National Ingredient

The soul of Burmese cuisine. Fermented green tea leaves tossed with fried garlic, toasted nuts, dried shrimp, and lime. Sour, savory, bitter, crunchy — all at once. Nothing else tastes like it on earth.

🥥
Coconut Broths
Ohn No Khao Swe

Rich, golden, fragrant — Burmese coconut soups are layered with turmeric, lemongrass, and fish sauce. Silky, warming, and utterly unlike Thai or Indian coconut curries.

🌶️
Dry Curries
Si Byan · Oil Returns

Burmese curries are oil-based, drier, and deeply caramelized. The “oil returns” technique coaxes umami depth from tomatoes, onions, and spices cooked low and slow.

🧆
Shan Tofu
Chickpea Curd · Not Soy

Made from chickpea flour, giving it a silkier, nuttier character. Golden fried, it becomes crisp outside and custard-soft inside. A revelation for anyone who thinks they don’t like tofu.

🍋
Sour Notes
Chin · The Brightener

Tamarind, lime, and fermented pastes thread sourness through Burmese cooking the way salt threads through Western cuisine. It lifts rich curries and gives salads their electric quality.

🫘
Legume Depth
Pe Gyaw · Split Peas

Split peas, yellow lentils, and dried beans appear everywhere — fried crisp as garnish, simmered into soups, or ground into noodles. They add earthy body that makes Burmese food so satisfying.

✶

The Burmese Table

How a Meal Works

In Myanmar, meals are served all at once — not in courses. A main curry arrives alongside multiple sides: a clear soup, pickled vegetables, a raw salad, steamed rice, and condiments. Everything is meant to be mixed and balanced at the table. Come hungry. Come curious.

Learn the Language

Burmese Kitchen
Glossary

You don’t need to know any of this to enjoy the food — but knowing a little changes everything. These are the building blocks of Myanmar’s culinary vocabulary. Use them to ask better questions when you visit.

Lahpet
Pronunciation: “Lah-peh”

Fermented tea leaves — the defining ingredient of Burmese cuisine. In Myanmar, lahpet symbolizes reconciliation; it was traditionally exchanged to end disputes between families. Served as a salad, snack, and gift.

Mohinga
Pronunciation: “Mo-hin-gah”

Myanmar’s national dish and the country’s most common breakfast: rice vermicelli in rich fish-and-lemongrass broth topped with crispy fritters. Every family and region makes it differently — a point of fierce regional pride.

Palatha
Pronunciation: “Pa-lah-tah”

A flaky, layered flatbread descended from Indian paratha but evolved into something entirely Burmese — butterier, more delicate, and griddle-cooked until blistered and golden. Multiple guests have called it the best bread they’ve ever eaten.

Ngapi
Pronunciation: “Nga-pee”

Fermented fish or shrimp paste — the backbone of Burmese savory cooking, used like salt and anchovy paste combined. Funky, intense, and completely transformed by cooking. The umami bedrock beneath every great Burmese dish.

Thoke
Pronunciation: “Thoh-kay”

The Burmese word for “salad” — though Burmese salads bear no resemblance to Western ones. They are hand-tossed, highly seasoned, layered with textures, and often served warm or at room temperature.

Si Byan
Pronunciation: “See-Byan”

Literally “oil returns” — the moment when oil separates from the sauce during curry cooking, signaling the onions, tomatoes, and spices have fully caramelized. The foundation of every great Burmese curry.

The Bread

Order the
Palatha

If there is one thing every first-time guest must order, it is the Palatha. Descended from Indian paratha but distinctly Burmese in execution — flaky, buttery, blistered on a hot griddle.

It tears like a croissant. It folds like a flatbread. It pairs with everything: curries, tea leaf salad, even a cup of Burmese milk tea. Multiple guests have called it the best bread they’ve ever eaten.

Pair it with the Pumpkin Chicken Stew for a beginner-perfect combination. Or dip it into the Chili Lamb gravy if you’re feeling bold.
